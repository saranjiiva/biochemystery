Historical Background: The Krebs Cycle

Discovery of the Cycle (1937)

Sir Hans Adolf Krebs proposed the "Citric Acid Cycle" in 1937. He demonstrated that the oxidation of carbohydrates involves a cyclic pathway rather than a linear one, a revolutionary concept at the time.

Initially rejected by the journal Nature, his paper described how pyruvate is oxidized in pigeon breast muscle. This work unified earlier findings on succinate, fumarate, and malate oxidation.

Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine (1953)

Awarded jointly to Hans Krebs "for his discovery of the citric acid cycle" and Fritz Lipmann "for his discovery of co-enzyme A and its importance for intermediary metabolism."

"I have tried to show that the intermediate stages of the breakdown of carbohydrate... form a cycle."

— Hans Krebs (1937)

Timeline of Discovery

1910s - 1920s

Early Foundations

Thunberg, Batelli, and Stern identify individual reactions (succinate → fumarate → malate).

1935 - 1937

Szent-Györgyi's Contribution

Albert Szent-Györgyi demonstrates that dicarboxylic acids (succinate, fumarate, malate, OAA) stimulate respiration catalytically.

1937

The "Tri-Carboxylic Acid" Cycle

Hans Krebs publishes the full cyclic mechanism in Enzymologia after Nature rejection. He links pyruvate oxidation to the regeneration of oxaloacetate.

1945 - 1950

Acetate Activation

Fritz Lipmann discovers Coenzyme A, clarifying how acetate enters the cycle as Acetyl-CoA.
